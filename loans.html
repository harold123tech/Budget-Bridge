Foundation

How Do Loans Work?

A loan is an agreement between you (the borrower) and a financial institution (the lender). They give you money now — you promise to pay it back later, in regular instalments, plus a fee for using that money. That fee is called interest.

🏛️

The Lender

Banks, credit unions, and online lenders provide the money. They earn a profit from the interest you pay. Lenders evaluate your creditworthiness before agreeing — if you have no credit history, they see you as a higher risk and may charge more or require a co-signer.

💰

Principal Balance

The principal is the original amount you borrowed — before any interest is added. Every repayment you make is split: part reduces the principal, part pays the interest. Early in a loan, most of your payment goes toward interest, not the balance itself.

📅

Repayment Timeline

Your loan term is the length of time you have to pay it back. Short terms (12–24 months) mean higher monthly payments but less total interest. Long terms (5–7 years) lower your monthly payment but cost significantly more in interest over time.

📊

Credit Score Impact

Every on-time payment builds your credit score. Every missed payment damages it. As a student or young adult, a starter credit card or small personal loan — paid on time, every time — is the fastest way to establish the credit history lenders want to see.

🔒

Secured vs Unsecured

Secured loans are backed by an asset you own (a car, a cash deposit). If you default, the lender takes the asset. Unsecured loans (most credit cards, student loans) are not backed by collateral — lenders rely on your promise and credit history alone.

🤝

Co-Signers

A co-signer is someone with an established credit history (often a parent) who agrees to be responsible for your debt if you cannot pay. Co-signers can help you qualify for loans you wouldn't get alone — but their credit is also at risk if you miss payments.

The average American carries $6,501 in credit card debt. Most of it exists not because of emergencies — but because of a lack of understanding of how interest compounds month over month. The section below shows you exactly what that looks like in real numbers.
