How Do Savings Accounts Work?

A savings account is a secure place to store money at a bank or credit union while earning interest over time. Here are the core concepts you need to know:

📈

Interest & APY

Banks pay you a percentage of your balance just for keeping money with them. APY (Annual Percentage Yield) is the real annual rate you earn, including compounding — the higher the APY, the faster your money grows.

🛡️

FDIC Insurance

Money in FDIC-insured accounts is protected by the federal government up to $250,000 per depositor, per bank. This means even if your bank fails, your savings are safe.

💧

Liquidity

Unlike investments, savings accounts let you access your money quickly and without penalty. Most accounts allow free withdrawals at any time, making them ideal for emergency funds.

📋

Minimum Balances

Some accounts require a minimum balance to open or to avoid monthly fees. Online banks often have no minimum requirements, while traditional banks may require $25–$500 to get started.

💸

Monthly Fees

Certain accounts charge a maintenance fee each month. These fees can often be waived by meeting a minimum balance or setting up direct deposit. Always check the fee structure before opening an account.

🔄

Compounding

Compounding means you earn interest on your interest. Most savings accounts compound daily or monthly. The more frequently interest compounds, the more you earn over time — even with the same APY.

Comparing Savings Account Types

Not all savings accounts are the same. Here is a breakdown of the four most common types and when each one makes the most sense for your goals.

Traditional

Traditional Savings Account

Offered by brick-and-mortar banks and credit unions. Easy to open, branch access included, but typically offers the lowest interest rates (0.01%–0.10% APY).

Best for: Beginners building their first savings habit or those who want in-person banking support.
High-Yield

High-Yield Savings Account

Typically offered by online banks with significantly higher APYs (4.00%–5.50%). No physical branches, but fully FDIC insured and accessible 24/7 online.

Best for: Anyone looking to grow their emergency fund or long-term savings faster with minimal effort.
Money Market

Money Market Account

A hybrid between a savings and checking account. Offers higher rates than traditional savings and often includes check-writing or debit card access. May require higher minimum balances.

Best for: Savers with a larger balance who want some spending flexibility without losing interest earnings.
CD

Certificate of Deposit (CD)

You lock your money in for a fixed term (3 months to 5 years) in exchange for a guaranteed, often higher interest rate. Early withdrawal results in a penalty fee.

Best for: Savers with money they won't need for a set period who want a guaranteed return with zero risk.
